Fostering Global Cooperation to Mitigate Climate Change

Climate change is a global crisis - it poses an existential threat to humanity. And it is happening quicker than we have feared. The latest IPCC report warns that it is ‘now or never’ if world is to stave off climate disaster. However, it is still a race we can win. While the climate change response has to come from individuals, public, and private sectors, a comprehensive global cooperation will be vital. For example, a nation's global declaration of net-zero commitments send political signals about future that can motivate domestic policy reforms, or transferring of financing and technologies to help developing countries, or cooperative initiatives among a few nations (e.g., climate clubs) targeted at overcoming barriers to actions either domestically or internationally.

Achieving long-term global cooperation between nations is a key challenge towards mitigating climate change, e.g., capping global temperature increase at 2 degrees Celsius. This poses a complex game-theoretic problem. Each nation can be modelled as a strategic actor that optimizes policy to achieve its socio-economic and climate objectives (which may be unknown to other nations). Nations interact, for example, they trade, communicate, or provide foreign investments. There is no central entity that effectively ensures that nations adhere to climate policy agreements. Cooperation is typically achieved through multilateral negotiations and agreements. As such, it is an open challenge to understand which types of economic and climate agreements best foster cooperation, and sustainably mitigate climate change, while allowing individual nations to meet their policy objectives.

To address this interdisciplinary challenge, the scientific community and policymakers need to find a solution. This competition aims to focus researchers worldwide on this challenging problem and take their findings to the policymakers who can benefit from them, thereby providing a bridge between the scientific and policy communities.

Competition Overview

What will you do?

The goal is to implement a negotiation strategy that will help regions to achieve their individual goals, but also (indirectly) lead to better climate outcomes.

You will use a climate-economic simulation with fictitious regions as independent AI agents. Each agent chooses how much to invest and mitigate climate change, sets international tariffs, limits exports, and imports goods.

The simulator runs for 100 years.

Regions also engage in multilateral negotiations.

The structural parameters are calibrated to real data.

To read more about the simulator, please refer to the paper and our Github repository.

Track 1

You will:

  • propose and implement multilateral agreements to augment the simulator,
  • train AI agents that negotiate with each other and optimize their utility, and
  • evaluate the learned policies and resulting economic and climate change metrics, e.g., global equality, productivity, temperature increase.

Scored by the number of other teams' best solutions are Pareto-dominated. Ties are broken by climate index score (higher = better). Teams should argue why their solution is feasible, technically sound, and attractive. For instance, from a game-theoretical perspective, good multilateral agreements might punish free-riders and might have to ensure that it’s difficult to “game” agreements with unrealistic behaviours. We expect the submissions to contain the following -

  • Modified code with negotiation protocol
  • RL agents trained on that protocol
  • A technical report containing plots and accompanying justification of suggested negotiation strategy
Please refer to the full submission guidelines for a full description of what is expected.

Track 2

In this track, we expect the participants to convey their findings in practically relevant context without going too technical. As we aim to bring the insights from the competition to the policymakers, we expect the entries in this track to be written for policymakers.

In addition to the requirements in the Track 1, we expect an additional submission

  • A 2500 word essay that summarizes the insights deliverable to policymaker jury

Track 3

We strive to closely simulate real-world dynamics. But, no simulator is perfect. Thus, we invite you to point out potential improvements and loopholes.
